Marie Curie: Pioneer of Radioactivity

Marie Curie in her laboratory, holding a flask—where she conducted pioneering research on radioactivity.

About Marie Curie

  • Birth: November 7, 1867, in Warsaw, Poland.
  • Education: Studied physics and mathematics at the Sorbonne in Paris.
  • Pioneering Research: First woman to win a Nobel Prize and the only person to win Nobel Prizes in two different scientific fields—Physics (1903) and Chemistry (1911).
  • Discovery: Discovered the radioactive elements polonium (named after Poland) and radium.
  • New Term: Coined the term "radioactivity" and developed techniques for isolating radioactive isotopes.
  • Medical Impact: Her work led to the development of X-ray machines and radiation therapy for cancer.
  • World War I Contribution: Developed mobile radiography units to assist battlefield surgeons during WWI.
  • Firsts:
    • First woman to teach at the Sorbonne.
    • First woman to win a Nobel Prize.
    • First person to win two Nobel Prizes.
  • Legacy: Curie’s research changed the face of science and medicine. She inspired generations of women in STEM.
  • Death: July 4, 1934, from aplastic anemia, likely caused by prolonged exposure to radiation.
